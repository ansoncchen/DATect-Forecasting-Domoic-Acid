\abstract{
Domoic acid (DA) contamination of shellfish is a recurring public health crisis along the U.S.\ Pacific Northwest coast, yet no quantitative forecasting system exists. We present DATect, the first machine learning system to generate one-week-ahead DA concentration forecasts for 10 monitoring sites spanning \SI{600}{\kilo\meter} of coastline, trained on 21 years of satellite, climate, and biological data (2003--2023). DATect uses per-site XGBoost or Random Forest models with nine data-leakage prevention mechanisms, evaluated on a fully independent test set ($n = 2{,}181$). All five Washington sites achieve high skill ($R^2 = 0.480$--$0.789$), while Oregon sites show lower skill due to sparser monitoring---a data quality limitation rather than a forecasting ceiling. Standard regression models detect fewer than 24\% of the spike transitions that trigger beach closures. A dedicated binary classifier using safe-baseline training detects 65.2\% of these transitions [95\% CI: 55\%--74\%], a 2.8$\times$ improvement over na\"ive persistence, demonstrating that actionable early warning requires a purpose-built transition detector. DATect is deployed as a publicly accessible web dashboard.
}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

% ── 1.1 DA Biology, Drivers, and Impacts ─────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Domoic Acid and \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} Blooms}
\label{sec:intro-biology}

Domoic acid (DA) is a potent neurotoxin produced by certain species of the marine diatom genus \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia}, primarily \textit{P.\ australis} and \textit{P.\ multiseries} \cite{Bates2018, Trainer2012}. As the causative agent of amnesic shellfish poisoning (ASP), DA bioaccumulates in filter-feeding shellfish and poses severe risks to human health, from gastrointestinal distress to permanent neurological damage and death \cite{Mos2001, Lefebvre2010, Wright1989}. Since the first documented ASP outbreak in 1987 \cite{Wright1989, Bates1998}, DA contamination has become a persistent and recurring threat along the U.S.\ Pacific coast, with particularly severe consequences in the Pacific Northwest (PNW) region of Washington and Oregon \cite{Lewitus2012, Horner1997, Trainer2012}.

The production of DA by \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} is driven by a complex interplay of environmental factors that makes forecasting exceptionally challenging. Unlike many freshwater cyanobacteria harmful algal blooms (HABs), where toxin production correlates relatively directly with cell abundance and nutrient loading \cite{Paerl2013}, DA production in \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} is triggered by physiological stress responses---particularly limitation of silicic acid and iron---rather than simply by the presence of blooms \cite{Trick2010, Marchetti2004, Lelong2012}. A given \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} bloom may produce no measurable DA or extremely high concentrations depending on nutrient stress conditions, making cell counts alone a poor predictor of toxicity \cite{Bates2018, Trainer2012, Sauvey2023}. This decoupling between cell abundance and toxin production represents one of the fundamental challenges in DA forecasting: a bloom may be present but benign, or a modest cell population under stress may produce dangerous toxin levels.

Coastal upwelling is the primary mechanism delivering nutrients that fuel \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} growth in the PNW. Wind-driven displacement of surface waters, largely from the California Current System, brings nutrient-rich deep water to the surface \cite{McKibben2017, Trainer2009}. The Biologically Effective Upwelling Transport Index (BEUTI) quantifies not only the physical intensity of upwelling but also the nutrient content of upwelled waters \cite{Jacox2018}, making it a particularly informative predictor for DA risk. This upwelling process is modulated by broader climate patterns: El Ni\~no events increase sea surface temperatures and disrupt nutrient dynamics by weakening upwelling \cite{Zhu2017}, while the Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) shifts between warm and cool phases over decades, influencing water temperature, nutrient distribution, and upwelling intensity \cite{Mantua1997, Wolter2011, DiLorenzo2016}. Critically, it is the relaxation of upwelling---not upwelling itself---that often triggers the onshore transport of toxic cells to shellfish beaches, creating a ``Goldilocks zone'' where moderate upwelling followed by relaxation produces the highest DA risk \cite{Trainer2002, Trainer2020}.

Specific oceanographic retention zones further concentrate blooms in the PNW. The Juan de Fuca eddy near northwest Washington and Heceta Bank off central Oregon create circulation patterns that retain \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} cells, allowing blooms to persist and intensify \cite{Trainer2009, Trainer2002, McCabe2023}. These hotspots have been associated with the most severe DA events in the region and contribute to the extreme spatial heterogeneity in DA dynamics along the coast.

The 2015 West Coast event exemplifies the scale of potential impacts. An unprecedented marine heatwave (``The Blob'') fueled the largest recorded DA outbreak along the North American west coast, with DA concentrations in razor clams exceeding the federal safety limit by over 700\% and causing \$97.5 million in Dungeness crab fishery losses alone \cite{McCabe2016, Bond2015, Moore2020, Trainer2020}. DA also poses ecological risks, including mass mortality events in marine mammals \cite{Scholin2000, Lefebvre2010, Lefebvre2016}. Climate change is expected to increase the frequency and geographic range of DA contamination events \cite{Wells2015, Litchman2023}, making the development of robust forecasting systems increasingly urgent.

These characteristics make DA arguably the most difficult marine biotoxin to predict. Compared to general cyanobacteria HABs in freshwater systems, DA forecasting faces four compounding challenges: (1) toxin production is decoupled from cell abundance, being triggered by physiological stress rather than bloom magnitude; (2) monitoring data are sparse and irregularly sampled, with most sites collecting only weekly or biweekly samples; (3) spatial heterogeneity is extreme across the 5\textdegree\ of latitude from southern Oregon to northern Washington; and (4) there is no simple nutrient-loading relationship, as the interaction of upwelling, relaxation, retention, and climate forcing creates a multi-scale, nonlinear system \cite{Bates2018, Trainer2012, McKibben2017}.

% ── 1.2 Existing Monitoring and Forecasting ──────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Existing Monitoring and Forecasting in the Pacific Northwest}
\label{sec:intro-monitoring}

Current approaches to DA risk management in the PNW are fundamentally reactive. Shellfish samples are collected by the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW), local Tribal Nations, and the Oregon Department of Fish and Wildlife (ODFW), then analyzed for DA concentrations by the Washington State Department of Health (WDOH) and the Oregon Department of Agriculture (ODA). Beaches with DA concentrations exceeding the U.S.\ Food and Drug Administration (FDA) regulatory limit of \SI{20}{\microgrampeg} in shellfish meat tissue are closed to harvest \cite{McCabe2023}. This system, while effective at protecting public health once contamination is detected, provides no advance warning: closures are announced only after toxin levels have already risen. The resulting abrupt closures can strand commercial fisheries with no time to adapt harvest plans, and recreational clam diggers often learn of closures only upon arrival at a beach.

The primary forecasting tool for the region is the NOAA Pacific Northwest Harmful Algal Bloom (PNW HAB) Bulletin, developed by the Pacific Marine Environmental Laboratory in collaboration with the University of Washington and regional shellfish managers \cite{McCabe2023}. Issued biweekly to monthly during peak bloom seasons, the Bulletin synthesizes a wide range of environmental data---including \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} cell counts, particulate DA levels, wind patterns, satellite imagery, and upwelling conditions---into a qualitative risk assessment. Scientists visually interpret these data and produce an educated assessment of bloom risk, categorized as low, medium, or high. In a self-assessment spanning 12 seasons (2017--2022), the Bulletin achieved six true positive and five true negative seasonal classifications with one false positive and no false negatives \cite{McCabe2023}. A recent economic analysis estimated that the Bulletin provides substantial value to fisheries management by helping optimize harvest timing \cite{Jin2024}. While valuable, this approach is inherently qualitative: it does not produce numerical DA concentration predictions, operates at seasonal rather than weekly resolution, and depends entirely on expert interpretation rather than automated quantitative models.

Oregon's coastal HAB monitoring program provides regular phytoplankton and toxin sampling along the Oregon coast \cite{McKibben2015}, while autonomous platforms such as the Environmental Sample Processor enable near real-time surveillance of harmful algae \cite{Moore2021}. The SoundToxins program provides citizen science monitoring of harmful phytoplankton in Puget Sound \cite{Trainer2023}, while LiveOcean, a ROMS-based physical-biogeochemical model developed at the University of Washington, simulates ocean conditions in the Salish Sea and adjacent waters but does not directly predict DA concentrations \cite{MacCready2021}. The California Harmful Algae Risk Mapping (C-HARM) system provides satellite-based nowcasts of \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} bloom probability along the California coast \cite{Anderson2016}, but does not extend to Washington or Oregon and targets bloom probability rather than toxin concentration.

A critical gap thus exists: \textbf{no quantitative DA concentration forecasting system has ever been developed for the Pacific Northwest}. All existing monitoring is reactive, and the sole forecasting tool provides qualitative risk categories, not numerical toxin predictions. This gap has persisted throughout the entire history of DA monitoring in the region.

% ── 1.3 Problem Statement ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Problem Statement and Contributions}
\label{sec:intro-problem}

This paper addresses the absence of quantitative DA forecasting in the PNW by developing DATect, the first system to produce site-specific, numerical DA concentration predictions for this region. We formalize the prediction task as follows: given environmental features available at time $t - 7$ days (the ``anchor date''), predict the DA concentration (\si{\microgrampeg}) at time $t$ for a specific monitoring site $s$ among 10 Pacific Coast locations.

This task presents five compounding challenges:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Sparse, irregular target variable}: DA measurements are collected approximately weekly but with frequent gaps, missingness, and inconsistent timing across sites.
    \item \textbf{High spatial variability}: The 10 monitoring sites span 5\textdegree\ of latitude ($\sim$\SI{600}{\kilo\meter}), with dramatically different oceanographic regimes, bloom histories, and DA dynamics.
    \item \textbf{Extreme value distribution}: DA measurements are dominated by zeros and low values, with rare but consequential spikes exceeding \SI{100}{\microgrampeg}.
    \item \textbf{Strict temporal integrity requirements}: Operational forecasting demands that no future information leak into predictions, requiring rigorous validation protocols.
    \item \textbf{Actionable, interpretable output}: Coastal managers need not only point predictions but also uncertainty estimates and risk categories aligned with regulatory thresholds.
\end{enumerate}

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{First quantitative DA forecasting for the PNW}: DATect produces numerical concentration predictions and four-level risk classifications, filling a gap that has persisted throughout the entire history of DA monitoring in the region.
    \item \textbf{Transition-focused spike detection}: A dedicated binary classifier using safe-baseline training provides genuine ML-derived advance warning for the below-to-above-threshold crossings that trigger beach closures (Section~\ref{sec:results-spike}).
    \item \textbf{Per-site ML architecture}: Per-site XGBoost or Random Forest models with custom hyperparameters, feature subsets, and prediction clipping for each of 10 coastal sites. Na\"ive persistence is evaluated as a standalone external baseline.
    \item \textbf{Data quality as the primary performance driver}: A clear performance split between Washington sites (consistent WDFW/Tribal monitoring, $R^2 = 0.480$--$0.789$) and Oregon sites (sparser monitoring, $R^2 \leq 0.290$) demonstrates that monitoring data density, not model architecture, is the binding constraint on forecast skill.
    \item \textbf{Rigorous leak-free validation}: Nine explicit data-leakage prevention mechanisms, including per-prediction temporal boundary assertions, addressing a pervasive problem in environmental machine learning \cite{Kapoor2023}.
    \item \textbf{Largest scope}: The longest temporal record (21 years) and broadest geographic span of any published quantitative DA machine learning study.
    \item \textbf{Publicly deployed web dashboard}: A React/FastAPI application deployed on Google Cloud Run, providing the first operational quantitative DA forecasting capability for the PNW.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\subsection{Quantitative Forecasting of Domoic Acid and \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia}}
\label{sec:rw-da-forecasting}

Despite decades of DA monitoring and multiple qualitative forecasting systems, quantitative computational approaches specifically targeting DA or \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} remain limited in number, geographic scope, and temporal extent \cite{Anderson2012, McGillicuddy2010}. No prior study has produced a quantitative DA concentration forecast for the PNW.

In the Galician R\'ias Baixas region of northwest Spain, Al\'aez et al.\ \cite{Alaez2021} applied Random Forest and AdaBoost classifiers to predict \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} bloom events, achieving approximately 85\% classification accuracy. Their study covered approximately five monitoring stations over roughly a decade of data, targeting bloom occurrence (binary classification) rather than DA concentration. Cusack et al.\ \cite{Cusack2015} modeled \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} events off southwest Ireland using environmental predictors, demonstrating the potential of empirical models for bloom forecasting in European waters. Additional studies have documented \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} dynamics and DA contamination in new regions, including the Gulf of Maine \cite{Clark2021} and southern Florida \cite{Schreiber2023}, reflecting the global expansion of DA monitoring efforts. In the California Current, Lane et al.\ \cite{Lane2009} developed a logistic regression model for predicting toxigenic \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} blooms in Monterey Bay using 8.3 years of monitoring data, achieving $\geq$75\% predictive accuracy with chlorophyll~$a$ and silicic acid as primary predictors.

The closest methodological parallel to DATect is the deep learning system developed by Grasso et al.\ \cite{Grasso2019} for forecasting paralytic shellfish toxin (PST) levels in coastal Maine. Using a convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on chemical data from routine monitoring sites, their system forecasts PST levels on a weekly basis with over 95\% prediction reliability within a one-week timeframe. However, this system targets a different toxin (PST from \textit{Alexandrium catenella}, not DA from \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia}), covers a smaller geographic area (approximately 10 sites along the Maine coast), and uses approximately five years of data.

The California Harmful Algae Risk Mapping (C-HARM) system \cite{Anderson2016} provides satellite-based nowcasts of \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} bloom probability and DA risk along the California coast. C-HARM integrates satellite imagery with ocean model output to produce gridded risk maps but operates as a nowcast (current conditions) rather than a forecast and does not extend to Washington or Oregon. Moreno et al.\ \cite{Moreno2022} developed a mechanistic process-based model of \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} and DA production for the California Current, representing a complementary approach to data-driven methods. Most recently, Brunson et al.\ \cite{Brunson2024} demonstrated molecular forecasting of DA using gene expression (dabA and sit1 coexpression) as a one-week-ahead predictor during a bloom in Monterey Bay, representing an emerging genomics-based approach that could eventually complement satellite- and ML-based systems like DATect.

\subsection{Machine Learning Methods for Harmful Algal Blooms}
\label{sec:rw-ml-habs}

Machine learning has been increasingly applied to HAB prediction across diverse systems. Cruz et al.\ \cite{Cruz2021} provide a comprehensive review of recent ML advances for forecasting HABs and shellfish contamination, identifying Random Forests, support vector machines (SVMs), and neural networks as the most commonly applied methods. Shin et al.\ \cite{Shin2024} further surveyed recent advances in algal bloom detection and prediction technology using machine learning, highlighting the growing role of explainable AI and ensemble methods. Hill et al.\ \cite{Hill2020} developed HABNet, a CNN-LSTM system for HAB detection and prediction using MODIS satellite data. Yu et al.\ \cite{Yu2021} demonstrated machine learning approaches for predicting coastal algal blooms using environmental factors. Manning et al.\ \cite{Manning2019} extended HAB forecast models to predict cyanobacteria blooms in western Lake Erie at multiple spatial scales.

Gradient boosting methods, pioneered by Friedman \cite{Friedman2001} and particularly the XGBoost implementation \cite{Chen2016}, have shown strong performance in environmental prediction tasks due to their ability to handle heterogeneous features, missing values, and nonlinear interactions. Random Forests \cite{Breiman2001} remain popular for ecological applications due to their interpretability and robustness. Ensemble methods that combine multiple model types have consistently outperformed single-model approaches in environmental forecasting \cite{Dietterich2000}. Ahn et al.\ \cite{Ahn2023} recently demonstrated that gradient boosting ensembles (XGBoost, LightGBM, CatBoost) combined with attention-based CNN-LSTM models achieve strong HAB forecasting performance, corroborating DATect's choice of XGBoost as a core ensemble component.

Remote sensing approaches using satellite imagery have also been applied to HAB detection. Gonz\'alez Vilas et al.\ \cite{GonzalezVilas2024} developed an SVM-based algorithm to detect \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} blooms from OLCI satellite data, while Blondeau-Patissier et al.\ \cite{BlondeauPatissier2014} reviewed ocean color remote sensing methods and instruments for marine monitoring relevant to HAB detection. Stumpf et al.\ \cite{Stumpf2009} demonstrated skill assessment methodologies for operational HAB forecast systems that inform evaluation frameworks for tools like DATect.

\subsection{Data Leakage in Environmental Machine Learning}
\label{sec:rw-leakage}

A growing body of work has highlighted the pervasive problem of data leakage in machine learning applications to environmental science. Kapoor and Narayanan \cite{Kapoor2023} demonstrated that data leakage affects a substantial fraction of published ML-based science papers, leading to inflated performance claims and irreproducible results. In temporal prediction tasks such as HAB forecasting, common sources of leakage include random train-test splits that violate temporal ordering, use of features computed from future data, and shared preprocessing across train and test sets \cite{Kaufman2012, Roberts2017, Bergmeir2012}. Proper out-of-sample evaluation for temporal data requires rolling-origin or expanding-window approaches rather than random holdouts \cite{Tashman2000}, and spatial autocorrelation in environmental data further complicates validation design \cite{Valavi2019}.

Many prior HAB forecasting studies employed random 80/20 train-test splits without temporal ordering \cite{Cruz2021, Shin2024}, which may have produced artificially inflated performance metrics. DATect addresses this explicitly through nine structural leakage-prevention mechanisms described in Section~\ref{sec:validation}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Study Area and Data Collection}
\label{sec:data-collection}

\subsubsection{Monitoring Sites}

DATect forecasts DA concentrations at 10 Pacific Coast monitoring sites spanning from Gold Beach, Oregon (42.38\textdegree\,N) to Kalaloch, Washington (47.59\textdegree\,N), a distance of approximately \SI{600}{\kilo\meter} along the coastline (Figure~\ref{fig:study-area}). Sites were selected for their comprehensive historical datasets, consistent sampling over a multi-year period, and spatial representation across the Washington--Oregon coast. Pacific razor clams (\textit{Siliqua patula}) are sampled at all sites due to their high susceptibility to DA and slow depuration rate (half-life of weeks to months), which makes them reliable bioindicators of DA exposure \cite{Drum1993, Wekell1994, McCabe2023}. Razor clams are an ideal sentinel species because they are sessile filter feeders that continuously accumulate DA during bloom events, and their slow depuration provides an integrated measure of recent DA exposure at each site.

Satellite data from MODIS-Aqua \cite{NASA2002} are retrieved as 8-day composites from the NOAA CoastWatch ERDDAP server for each site's local bounding box. The use of 8-day composites mitigates the effects of cloud cover, which frequently obstructs satellite observations in the PNW---a region that experiences cloud cover on approximately 70\% of days during the peak DA season. A 7-day processing buffer is enforced to simulate operational satellite data availability delays.

Climate indices include the Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) \cite{Mantua1997}, the Oceanic Ni\~no Index (ONI) \cite{Wolter2011}, and the Biologically Effective Upwelling Transport Index (BEUTI) \cite{Jacox2018}. BEUTI is provided at discrete 1\textdegree\ latitude bands from 31\textdegree\,N to 47\textdegree\,N; site-specific values are obtained via inverse distance weighting (IDW) interpolation to each site's exact latitude. PDO and ONI are subject to a 2-month reporting delay to reflect their official publication schedule; BEUTI, available daily, uses a 7-day buffer. Columbia River discharge data from the USGS gauge at The Dalles, Oregon, serves as a proxy for freshwater input influencing coastal salinity and nutrient dynamics, with the strongest signal at northern sites above 46.5\textdegree\,N. The Columbia River is the largest river by discharge volume entering the Pacific along the U.S.\ West Coast, and its plume can extend over 100~km offshore during high-flow periods, substantially altering coastal nutrient dynamics.

DA measurements and \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} cell counts were obtained through public records requests to WDOH, WDFW, ODFW, and the Olympic Region Harmful Algal Bloom (ORHAB) Partnership. Two distinct CSV formats are handled: Washington data uses separate columns for harvest month/date/year with DA in \si{\microgrampeg}, while Oregon data uses a single date column with DA as ``Domoic Result.'' Measurements reported as ``$<$1'' (below detection limit) are imputed as $0.5$~\si{\microgrampeg} (half the detection threshold), a standard practice for left-censored environmental data. These biological measurements are irregularly spaced, reflecting operational sampling schedules that intensify during suspected bloom events and relax during quiescent periods.

\subsection{Data Engineering}
\label{sec:data-engineering}

\subsubsection{Weekly Aggregation and Gap Handling}

All data are aggregated to Monday-aligned ISO weeks. DA concentrations within each week are aggregated using the \textit{maximum} operation---a conservative choice that preserves worst-case readings for public health relevance. When multiple measurements exist within a single week (common during active bloom monitoring), the maximum ensures that the highest detected toxin level is retained.

Gaps in DA and \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} time series are filled using biologically motivated \textit{exponential decay} interpolation, reflecting the natural depuration dynamics of DA in shellfish tissue:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y}(t + \Delta t) = y(t) \cdot e^{-\lambda \Delta t}
\label{eq:decay}
\end{equation}
where $y(t)$ is the last observed value, $\Delta t$ is the gap duration in weeks, and $\lambda$ is the species-specific decay rate. For DA, $\lambda_{\text{DA}} = 0.2$ per week (half-life $\approx 3.5$ weeks) with a maximum interpolation gap of 2 weeks; for \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} cell counts, $\lambda_{\text{PN}} = 0.3$ per week (half-life $\approx 2.3$ weeks) with a maximum gap of 4 weeks. Gaps exceeding these thresholds are filled with zero, assuming non-detection. This approach is scientifically preferable to linear interpolation for two reasons. First, it reflects the biological reality of toxin depuration in shellfish \cite{Drum1993, Wekell1994, Bricelj1998}---linear interpolation would imply a constant rate of change between measurements, which is physically unrealistic for a biological decay process. Second, and critically for temporal integrity, exponential decay propagates information \textit{only forward in time} from the last known observation. Linear interpolation between two measurements would require the \textit{future} measurement to compute intermediate values, introducing temporal data leakage into the gap-filled training targets. Exponential decay avoids this entirely: each gap-filled value depends solely on the most recent past observation and the elapsed time since that observation.

\textbf{Critical distinction: augmented training, raw-only evaluation.} Exponential decay interpolation serves two purposes: (1)~constructing the \textit{environmental feature grid}---the weekly time series from which satellite, climate, and \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} features are drawn, and (2)~\textit{augmenting the training set} with gap-filled DA values. Training samples include both raw measurements and gap-filled DA values, providing approximately 5$\times$ more training data per site than raw-only training. Where a real measurement exists, it takes priority over the gap-filled value; gap-filled values are used only for weeks without a real sample. This augmentation is controlled by the \texttt{USE\_INTERPOLATED\_TRAINING} configuration flag.

Critically, \textit{test} samples remain restricted to weeks with actual raw DA measurements, ensuring all evaluation metrics reflect predictions of real toxin concentrations. Because the gap-filled values use forward-only exponential decay, they do not introduce temporal leakage into the training set. However, persistence features (\texttt{last\_observed\_da\_raw}, \texttt{weeks\_since\_last\_spike}) and the na\"ive persistence baseline are computed exclusively from real observations as a matter of methodological purity: these features should reflect what a coastal manager would actually \textit{know} from field sampling, not synthetic model-generated estimates. A \texttt{\_is\_interpolated} marker column tracks which training rows use gap-filled targets, enabling this selective filtering. This design provides the statistical benefits of a larger training set while ensuring that persistence signals and evaluation metrics reflect actual field measurements.

\subsubsection{Observation-Order Lag Features}
\label{sec:obs-order-lags}

DATect uses \textit{observation-order lag features}---the $N$th most recent actual observation strictly before the test date---rather than fixed time-grid shifts (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-engineering}). However, ablation shows these are largely redundant with the persistence feature at the one-week horizon ($\Delta R^2 = -0.001$; Section~\ref{sec:results-ablation}).

\subsubsection{Derived Environmental Features}

Six derived features encoding domain knowledge---including a marine heatwave flag, BEUTI nonlinear terms, PDO--ONI phase coherence, \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} transforms, fluorescence efficiency, and a K490 quadratic term---were explored during development (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-engineering}). Individual feature ablation (Table~\ref{tab:feature-ablation}) confirmed that all produce negligible impact ($|\Delta R^2| \leq 0.005$), and all have been removed from the final pipeline.

\subsubsection{Persistence Features and Rolling Statistics}

Two persistence features are computed from raw DA observations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \texttt{last\_observed\_da\_raw}: Forward-fill of the most recent DA measurement
    \item \texttt{weeks\_since\_last\_spike}: Time since last DA $> \SI{20}{\microgrampeg}$ event
\end{itemize}

Rolling statistics (mean, standard deviation, and maximum) are computed over windows of 4, 8, and 12 weeks for each site. Rolling means for 8- and 12-week windows and all rolling standard deviation features have negligible importance and are dropped; only the 4-week rolling mean/maximum and 8-/12-week maximum carry meaningful signal. To prevent the current week's DA from leaking into its own features, rolling statistics are computed on a \texttt{shift(1)} version of the persistence series---that is, the rolling window begins from the \textit{prior} week's forward-filled DA value, not the current week's. This one-row offset ensures that a test point's rolling features reflect only DA history available \textit{before} the prediction date, even if the test point itself corresponds to a raw measurement week.

\subsubsection{Temporal Features and Preprocessing}

Cyclical temporal features (sine/cosine encodings of day-of-year and month) capture seasonal bloom patterns without year-boundary discontinuities. Additional temporal encodings (week-of-year, bloom season flag, linear trend) were explored but confirmed negligible and removed (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-engineering}). All numeric features undergo median imputation for missing values followed by min-max scaling to $[0, 1]$ using scikit-learn \cite{Pedregosa2011}.

\subsection{Model Architecture}
\label{sec:model-selection}

\subsubsection{Two-Model ML Ensemble}

DATect employs a weighted two-model ML ensemble for regression:
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = w_{\text{XGB}} \cdot \hat{y}_{\text{XGB}} + w_{\text{RF}} \cdot \hat{y}_{\text{RF}}
\label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}
where $w_{\text{XGB}} + w_{\text{RF}} = 1$, and the weights are site-specific (Table~\ref{tab:ensemble-config}). Na\"ive persistence is computed for every prediction and reported as a standalone external baseline (Section~\ref{sec:results-overall}), enabling a direct assessment of how much skill the ML ensemble adds beyond simple autocorrelation.

\textbf{XGBoost} \cite{Chen2016}: A gradient boosted tree model using the histogram-based training method (\texttt{tree\_method=hist}) with global base hyperparameters ($n_{\text{estimators}} = 400$, max depth $= 6$, learning rate $= 0.05$, subsample $= 0.85$, colsample\_bytree $= 0.85$, colsample\_bylevel $= 0.8$, $\alpha = 0.1$, $\lambda = 1.0$, $\gamma = 0.1$, min\_child\_weight $= 3$) and per-site overrides (Appendix~\ref{app:hyperparams}). During retrospective evaluation, per-anchor hyperparameter selection uses a calibration fraction of 30\% of training data (maximum 20 rows), searching over a site-specific candidate grid of 1--3 configurations; in real-time deployment, per-site hyperparameters are used directly without per-anchor tuning. XGBoost's native handling of missing values and its L1/L2 regularization make it well-suited for the noisy, gap-filled environmental feature space.

\textbf{Random Forest} \cite{Breiman2001}: An ensemble of decision trees with global parameters ($n_{\text{estimators}} = 400$, max depth $= 12$, min samples split $= 5$, min samples leaf $= 3$, max features $= 0.85$). A conservative variant (max depth $= 6$, min samples split $= 10$, max features $= 0.5$) is used at sites with historically low skill. Random Forest's bagging approach provides complementary diversity to XGBoost's sequential boosting.

\textbf{Na\"ive persistence baseline}: Predicts the most recent raw DA measurement at or before the anchor date. This simple baseline tests whether ML models add value beyond the assumption that ``tomorrow's DA is similar to today's.'' Given the slow depuration kinetics of DA in razor clams \cite{Drum1993}, this baseline is non-trivial and represents a genuinely competitive approach at sites with strong temporal autocorrelation.

\subsubsection{Per-Site Customization}

A distinguishing feature of DATect is that each of the 10 monitoring sites receives a custom configuration encompassing: (1) XGBoost and Random Forest hyperparameter overrides, (2) a curated feature subset, (3) ensemble weights $(w_{\text{XGB}}, w_{\text{RF}})$, and (4) prediction clipping parameters. This per-site approach acknowledges that the 10 sites exhibit fundamentally different DA dynamics due to their distinct oceanographic settings, sampling histories, and bloom characteristics. A single global model would be forced to compromise across these diverse regimes, while per-site customization allows each site's model to specialize.

All low-skill and moderate sites require heavy regularization and aggressive prediction clipping due to limited data or unusual dynamics. Na\"ive persistence remains available as a standalone model type for direct baseline comparison (Section~\ref{sec:results-overall}).

\textbf{Configuration selection procedure.} Per-site ensemble weights, feature subsets, and hyperparameter overrides were selected through an iterative manual process informed by both domain knowledge and preliminary evaluation results. For each site, XGBoost and Random Forest were evaluated independently using the leak-free retrospective protocol described in Section~\ref{sec:validation}, using a \textit{development} test set (seed = 42, 20\% sampling fraction). Ensemble weights were set proportionally to each component's relative per-site performance; sites where one model clearly dominated converged to a single-model configuration, while sites with comparable ML model performance received split weights. Na\"ive persistence was also evaluated independently as an external baseline but was excluded from the ensemble blend in order to provide a clean separation between ML ensemble skill and raw autocorrelation. Feature subsets were curated by removing feature groups that degraded site-specific performance, prioritizing parsimony at data-sparse sites to reduce overfitting.

To prevent optimistic bias from evaluating on the same data used for configuration selection, all results reported in this paper use an \textit{independent} test set drawn with a different random seed (seed = 123) and a larger sampling fraction (40\%). This independent evaluation set was never used during any stage of per-site configuration development, ensuring that reported metrics reflect genuine out-of-sample performance. During retrospective evaluation, per-anchor hyperparameter selection within each test point additionally uses a separate calibration fraction (30\% of training data) that is independent of both test sets.

\subsubsection{Competitor Models}

Two additional models are included as competitors (not baselines):

\textbf{Ridge regression} \cite{Hoerl1970}: L2-regularized linear regression ($\alpha = 1.0$) using the full feature set, representing the best linear model.

\textbf{Logistic regression}: For classification tasks, using an L-BFGS solver with $C = 1.0$ and 1,000 maximum iterations.

\subsubsection{Confidence Intervals}

Prediction uncertainty is estimated via \textbf{quantile regression} \cite{Koenker1978}, which trains separate XGBoost models with the \texttt{reg:quantile} objective at specified percentiles---the 5th, 50th, and 95th for real-time forecasts (90\% confidence interval) and the 10th, 50th, and 90th for retrospective evaluation (80\% confidence interval, chosen for tighter calibration assessment). In ensemble mode, the quantile XGBoost predictions are blended with point-estimate Random Forest and na\"ive predictions using the site-specific ensemble weights, producing a hybrid confidence interval that reflects both quantile uncertainty and ensemble diversity.

\subsection{Validation and Scientific Integrity}
\label{sec:validation}

\subsubsection{Leak-Free Validation Protocol}

DATect enforces nine explicit data-leakage prevention mechanisms, summarized in Table~\ref{tab:leakage-safeguards}. These address the pervasive problem of temporal data leakage in environmental ML \cite{Kapoor2023, Roberts2017}. Unlike many published environmental ML studies that rely on random train-test splits, DATect enforces strict chronological ordering at every stage of the pipeline.

\begin{table}[H]
\caption{Nine data-leakage prevention mechanisms enforced in DATect. Each safeguard is structural (enforced in code) rather than procedural.}
\label{tab:leakage-safeguards}
\small
\begin{tabular}{clp{7cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{\#} & \textbf{Safeguard} & \textbf{Implementation} \\
\midrule
1 & Chronological split & Training data restricted to date $\leq$ anchor date \\
2 & Forward-only gap-filling & Exponential decay propagates only from past observations; unlike linear interpolation, gap-filled values never depend on future measurements \\
3 & Observation-order lags & Past-only shifts on raw DA measurements \\
4 & Satellite processing delay & 7-day buffer enforced at data ingestion \\
5 & Climate reporting delay & 2-month buffer for PDO, ONI, monthly anomalies \\
6 & Fixed category thresholds & DA risk bins (5, 20, 40 \si{\microgrampeg}) are regulatory constants, not derived from training data \\
7 & Persistence recomputation & Persistence features recomputed per test point using only training data \\
8 & Fresh model per test point & New model trained for each prediction; no shared state \\
9 & Runtime assertion & \texttt{verify\_no\_data\_leakage()} called on every prediction \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The central concept is the \textit{anchor date}, defined as $t_{\text{anchor}} = t_{\text{forecast}} - 7$ days. All training data must satisfy date $\leq t_{\text{anchor}}$, and the test date must satisfy $t_{\text{test}} > t_{\text{anchor}}$. This is verified by an assertion function called on every single prediction, raising an error if violated. Safeguard \#7 (fresh model per test point) is particularly aggressive---rather than training a single model and applying it to multiple test points, DATect retrains from scratch for each prediction, ensuring that no information from future test points can influence earlier predictions through model state.

\subsubsection{Retrospective Evaluation}

Model performance is evaluated through leak-free retrospective validation using a two-stage protocol. Per-site configurations (ensemble weights, feature subsets, hyperparameter overrides) were developed using a \textit{development} test set (seed = 42, 20\% sampling fraction). All results reported in this paper are evaluated on an \textit{independent} test set drawn with a different random seed (seed = 123) and a larger sampling fraction (40\%), ensuring that no reported metric was influenced by the configuration selection process. For each site, 40\% of raw DA measurements are sampled as candidate test points, subject to a history requirement that $\geq 33\%$ of the site's total record must precede the test date. At each test point, a fresh model is trained on all data up to the anchor date, predictions are generated, and metrics are computed. Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals ($B = 10{,}000$; percentile method \cite{Efron1979}) are reported for key metrics to quantify estimation uncertainty, particularly at sites with limited sample sizes.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:forecast} presents the pseudocode for a single forecast generation.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{forecast\_date $t$, site $s$, model\_type}
\KwOut{Prediction $\hat{y}$, confidence interval, risk category}
$t_{\text{anchor}} \gets t - 7$ days\;
Load feature frame for site $s$\;
$\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}} \gets \{(x_i, y_i) : \text{date}_i \leq t_{\text{anchor}}\}$\;
$x_{\text{test}} \gets$ environmental features at or before $t_{\text{anchor}}$\;
Recompute persistence features from $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ only\;
Build observation-order lag features from $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ only\;
Apply preprocessing (median impute $\to$ MinMax scale)\;
\texttt{verify\_no\_data\_leakage}($\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}, t, t_{\text{anchor}}$)\;
$m \gets$ site-specific model type for $s$ \tcp*{XGBoost or Random Forest}
Train model $m$ on $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ with site-specific hyperparameters\;
$\hat{y} \gets m(x_{\text{test}})$\;
$\hat{y}_{\text{Naive}} \gets$ most recent DA in $\mathcal{D}_{\text{train}}$ \tcp*{external baseline only}
Apply prediction clipping (site-specific quantile and/or hard max)\;
Compute confidence interval via quantile regression\;
Classify $\hat{y}$ into risk category (Low/Moderate/High/Extreme)\;
\Return{$\hat{y}$, CI, category, feature importance}\;
\caption{DATect Single Forecast Generation}
\label{alg:forecast}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metrics}

Regression performance is assessed using the coefficient of determination ($R^2$), mean absolute error (MAE), and root mean squared error (RMSE). Classification performance uses accuracy, per-class precision, recall, and F1 score. Spike detection (binary DA $> \SI{20}{\microgrampeg}$) is evaluated using the F2 score, which weights recall twice as heavily as precision ($\beta = 2$), and a recall-optimized operating point ($p \geq 0.10$), reflecting the asymmetric cost structure where a missed toxic event (potential human illness) carries far greater consequence than a false alarm (unnecessary precautionary advisory).

DATect is deployed as a publicly accessible web dashboard providing real-time forecasting and retrospective analysis capabilities. Full architectural details, deployment configuration, and visualization descriptions are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:webapp}.

\subsection{Spike Transition Detection}
\label{sec:results-spike}

While regression metrics characterize average forecasting skill, the operationally critical question is whether DATect can anticipate \emph{spike transitions}---the moment DA crosses from below to at or above the \SI{20}{\microgrampeg} regulatory threshold. These below-to-above crossings are exactly the events that trigger beach closures, and early detection would provide the advance warning that current reactive monitoring cannot.

\subsubsection{Environmental Lead Signals}
\label{sec:results-env-lead}

Analysis of 385 spike transition events across all 10 sites reveals that environmental features change detectably in the 1--4 weeks \emph{before} DA crosses the regulatory threshold. The Pacific Decadal Oscillation (PDO) showed the strongest pre-spike signal with rank-biserial effect size $r = 0.50$, followed by SST anomaly ($r = 0.40$), BEUTI ($r = 0.32$), and Columbia River discharge ($r = 0.27$, Washington sites only). All seven environmental features tested showed statistically significant deviations from baseline ($p < 0.05$, Wilcoxon signed-rank test) during the four weeks preceding spike transitions. These lead signals confirm that the environmental information needed for anticipatory spike detection exists in the feature set.

\subsubsection{Regression Threshold Limitations}
\label{sec:results-threshold-limit}

Despite this, the regression ensemble's spike transition recall at the standard \SI{20}{\microgrampeg} decision threshold is poor: only 12.4\% of transitions are correctly predicted as spikes ($n = 89$ transitions, seed = 123 independent test set). Na\"ive persistence achieves 23.6\%. The ensemble \emph{does} predict elevated values before spikes---mean predicted DA for transition events is \SI{13.1}{\microgrampeg}---but the conservative bias inherent in MSE-optimized regression prevents predictions from crossing the regulatory threshold. A threshold sensitivity analysis confirms this: lowering the decision boundary from 20 to \SI{12}{\microgrampeg} increases transition recall substantially while maintaining acceptable precision.

\subsubsection{Transition-Focused Binary Classifier}
\label{sec:results-spike-classifier}

To exploit the pre-spike environmental signals without the regression's conservative bias, we developed a dedicated binary spike classifier trained alongside the regression ensemble. The classifier uses a \emph{safe-baseline} training strategy: it trains only on observations where the previous DA measurement was below \SI{20}{\microgrampeg}, forcing it to learn from environmental and rolling features rather than simple persistence. This prevents the classifier from relying on ``last DA was already high'' as its primary signal---a strategy that would detect ongoing spikes but miss the operationally critical below-to-above transitions.

Table~\ref{tab:spike-transition} compares spike transition detection across approaches on the independent test set (seed = 123, $n = 2{,}181$, 89 transitions).

Per-site customization is the most impactful component ($\Delta R^2 = -0.158$), confirming that the 10 monitoring sites exhibit fundamentally different DA dynamics that cannot be captured by a single global model configuration. The $R^2 = 0.256$ achieved without per-site customization on the development set (seed = 42) reflects the cost of using a single global model; the independent test set $R^2 = 0.204$ (seed = 123, Table~\ref{tab:model-comparison}) is lower still, driven primarily by the poor Oregon site performance on the larger sample.

Interpolated training ($\Delta R^2 = -0.029$) provides a modest contribution, while observation-order lags and derived environmental features show negligible standalone impact. The small effect of lag removal likely reflects redundancy with the persistence features (\texttt{last\_observed\_da\_raw}), which capture similar recent-DA information. The negligible impact of derived features---removing \texttt{pn\_log} actually produces a marginal $R^2$ \emph{increase} of 0.001---suggests that the tree-based models can implicitly learn the nonlinear relationships that derived features encode explicitly. Note that ablation $\Delta R^2$ values are reported as point estimates without confidence intervals, as each experiment requires a separate full retrospective evaluation on Hyak and prediction-level data were not cached. The small magnitudes for observation-order lags ($-0.001$) and derived features ($+0.001$) are within typical sampling variability and should be interpreted as indicating negligible rather than precisely measured contributions.

The difference between development set $R^2$ (0.414, seed = 42) and independent test set $R^2$ (0.204, seed = 123) quantifies the combined effect of per-site configuration tuning and the sampling of different test points across the two seeds. A gap of 0.210 is substantial; however, it is driven almost entirely by the four low-skill Oregon sites (Newport $R^2 = -0.299$, Cannon Beach $R^2 = -0.044$, Coos Bay $R^2 = -0.039$, Gold Beach $R^2 = 0.041$), which have near-zero or negative $R^2$ and disproportionately lower the global average on the larger $n = 2{,}181$ independent test set. Washington sites individually achieve $R^2 = 0.480$--$0.789$, consistent with the dev-set range. This gap is therefore a genuine data-quality limitation at Oregon sites rather than evidence of overfitting at Washington sites.

A fine-grained per-feature ablation (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-ablation}) confirms that no individual environmental feature exceeds noise-level impact ($|\Delta R^2| \leq 0.005$); removing K490 actually \textit{improves} $R^2$ by 0.001, and removing all low-importance features simultaneously costs only $\Delta R^2 = -0.004$. Predictive skill derives primarily from DA persistence history and architectural choices, not environmental feature engineering. Features that do contribute---SST, BEUTI, PDO, ONI, Columbia River discharge---operate through raw input values, not derived transformations.

Feature importance analysis (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-importance}) confirms that persistence and observation-order lag features dominate across all sites, with BEUTI, SST, PDO, and discharge contributing meaningfully at Washington sites. A key caveat: high feature importance does not imply high marginal contribution---lag features rank highly in model splits but are largely redundant with \texttt{last\_observed\_da\_raw} ($\Delta R^2 = -0.001$ when removed). Skill derives overwhelmingly from DA persistence history and architectural choices, not environmental feature engineering.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Explanations and Analysis}
\label{sec:discussion-analysis}

\subsubsection{Data Source Split and Performance Gradient}

The striking performance gradient---five high-skill Washington sites versus lower-skill Oregon sites---is confirmed on the fully independent test set (seed = 123), ruling out per-site configuration overfitting as an explanation. All five Washington sites achieve $R^2 = 0.480$--$0.789$, while three Oregon sites (Cannon Beach, Coos Bay, Newport) have $R^2 < 0$ and the best-performing Oregon site (Clatsop Beach) achieves only $R^2 = 0.290$. We attribute this split primarily to differences between the two monitoring programs: Washington data originates from the Washington Department of Fish and Wildlife (WDFW) and Tribal monitoring programs, which provide higher sampling density and more temporally consistent records \cite{McCabe2023}, while Oregon data originates from a separate state program with sparser and less regular sampling. Machine learning models require sufficient training density to learn lag and rolling features reliably; irregular Oregon sampling reduces the effective information content of these features. The Columbia River discharge feature (\texttt{discharge}) also provides an informative environmental signal specific to the Washington sites north of the river mouth, further contributing to the performance gap.

We cannot rule out that oceanographic differences also play a role---Washington's Juan de Fuca eddy retention zone may produce more persistent DA dynamics that are intrinsically easier to forecast \cite{Trainer2009}---but the data quality explanation is the more parsimonious primary cause. Oregon site performance should therefore be interpreted as a current data limitation rather than a fundamental forecasting ceiling: better-sampled Oregon data would likely yield substantially improved skill.

Coos Bay ($R^2 = -0.039$) and Newport ($R^2 = -0.299$) have negative $R^2$, indicating that the signal-to-noise ratio in the available data is too low for any model to outperform the unconditional mean. Gold Beach ($R^2 = 0.041$) shows marginally positive but negligible skill, and Cannon Beach ($R^2 = -0.044$) is near zero. These four Oregon sites collectively pull down the global $R^2$ from the Washington-only range of 0.480--0.789 to the overall test-set value of 0.204.

\subsubsection{The Persistence Signal and ML Contribution}

The strong autocorrelation in DA at Washington sites reflects a genuine biological phenomenon: DA depuration from razor clam tissue occurs slowly, with half-lives measured in weeks to months \cite{Drum1993, Bricelj1998}. DA concentrations at time $t$ are therefore highly correlated with concentrations at time $t - 7$ days during sustained bloom events. Na\"ive persistence (reported here as an external standalone baseline) exploits this autocorrelation directly, achieving high $R^2$ at Washington sites in retrospective evaluation.

By excluding na\"ive persistence from the ML ensemble, DATect enables a clean separation: the ensemble $R^2$ represents the contribution of XGBoost and Random Forest \textit{alone}, without any persistence component inflating the metric. At Oregon sites, where na\"ive persistence has negative $R^2$ on the development set (Gold Beach: $-0.858$; Coos Bay: $-0.286$; Newport: $-1.572$ [dev set, seed = 42]), persistence actively degrades performance, confirming that the ML ensemble's Oregon results already reflect pure ML skill. The overall na\"ive persistence $R^2 = -0.426$ on the independent test set further confirms this. The geographic split thus reflects two distinct forecasting regimes: autocorrelation-rich in Washington (where both ML models and naive persistence perform well), and highly variable in Oregon where persistence is counterproductive and ML models provide all available skill.

The genuine ML contribution \textit{beyond} persistence is most clearly demonstrated by the transition-focused binary classifier, which is trained without any persistence component and still outperforms na\"ive persistence by 2.8$\times$ on the operationally critical low-to-high crossings (Section~\ref{sec:results-spike}).

The overall $R^2$ of 0.204 should be interpreted in context: DATect is evaluated under strict leak-free conditions where every prediction is made with a fresh model trained only on past data, and reported on an independent test set separate from all configuration-tuning data. Many published environmental ML studies use random train-test splits that allow future data to contaminate training sets, which can inflate $R^2$ by 0.1--0.3 or more \cite{Kapoor2023}. DATect's metrics therefore represent a conservative lower bound on actual performance. The five Washington coast sites individually achieve $R^2$ values of 0.480--0.789, which are strong by any standard for week-ahead environmental forecasting. Oregon sites drag the global average down substantially: Newport ($R^2 = -0.299$), Cannon Beach ($R^2 = -0.044$), Coos Bay ($R^2 = -0.039$), and Gold Beach ($R^2 = 0.041$) all contribute near-zero or negative skill, reflecting data sparsity rather than a fundamental forecasting barrier.

\subsubsection{Tree-Based Models vs.\ Linear Regression}

The aggregate $R^2$ of the per-site ML models (0.204) and Ridge regression (0.202) are nearly identical, which raises the question of whether tree-based models provide genuine benefit. An important caveat is that the Ridge baseline in DATect is not a simple linear model: it receives the same per-site feature subsets, per-site prediction clipping, and full persistence/lag feature set as the tree-based models---the only difference is the model type itself. Feature importance analysis reveals that 85\% of Ridge's signal derives from DA persistence and rolling features, which exhibit an approximately linear relationship with future DA concentrations during sustained blooms. Given this near-linear structure, the similar aggregate $R^2$ is expected rather than surprising.

Despite this, tree-based models provide consistent advantages in prediction error: the ensemble achieves lower MAE at 9 of 10 sites (overall \SI{6.42}{\microgrampeg} vs.\ \SI{6.76}{\microgrampeg}, a 5\% reduction), with the largest improvements at the most challenging sites---Newport ($-1.70$~\si{\microgrampeg}), Gold Beach ($-0.53$~\si{\microgrampeg}), and Clatsop Beach ($-0.32$~\si{\microgrampeg}). Per-site $R^2$ differences are modest but site-dependent: the ensemble leads most clearly at Clatsop Beach ($\Delta R^2 = +0.075$), where nonlinear feature interactions are strongest.

For spike detection (DA $\geq$ \SI{20}{\microgrampeg}), however, Ridge actually detects more events (68.1\% vs.\ 55.8\%) because both models share the same per-site prediction clipping, which suppresses extreme predictions to improve typical-case MAE at the cost of spike sensitivity. Since Ridge's raw (pre-clipped) predictions tend to be slightly higher---reflecting its less regularized fit---it retains more spike signals after clipping.

This analysis underscores two key findings. First, the dominant contribution to forecast skill comes from \textit{per-site feature engineering and prediction postprocessing} rather than model type: both tree-based and linear models benefit equally from per-site feature selection and clipping. This is consistent with the component ablation (Table~\ref{tab:ablation}), where removing per-site customization costs $\Delta R^2 = -0.158$---by far the largest single factor. Second, the spike detection problem cannot be solved by any regression approach; the transition-focused binary classifier (Section~\ref{sec:results-spike}) provides the operationally critical advance warning that neither tree-based nor linear regression can deliver.

\subsubsection{Spike Detection: From Regression to Binary Classification}

The contrast between overall regression performance and spike transition recall (12.4\% at the \SI{20}{\microgrampeg} threshold) reveals a fundamental limitation of MSE-optimized regression for safety-critical event detection. Regression models are trained to minimize average squared error across all samples, creating a conservative bias that pulls extreme predictions toward the mean. While the ensemble correctly identifies elevated DA trends before spikes (mean predicted DA for transitions is \SI{13.1}{\microgrampeg}), the MSE objective prevents crossing the \SI{20}{\microgrampeg} threshold.

The transition-focused binary classifier addresses this by reframing the problem: instead of predicting ``how much DA?'', it predicts ``will DA cross the regulatory threshold?''. The safe-baseline training strategy---training only on observations where the previous DA was below \SI{20}{\microgrampeg}---is critical because it prevents the classifier from learning the trivial signal that ``DA is already high, so it stays high.'' This forces reliance on environmental precursors (PDO, SST anomaly, BEUTI relaxation) and gradual toxin accumulation signals (rolling DA maximum), which are exactly the features that carry information about \emph{impending} transitions.

The 65.2\% transition recall with 33.9\% precision represents a deliberate trade-off: in shellfish safety, missing a true spike (false negative) carries far greater cost---potential human illness---than a false alarm (unnecessary precautionary advisory). The low probability threshold ($p \geq 0.10$) reflects this asymmetric cost structure, consistent with the F2 metric's emphasis on recall over precision.

In practical terms, the 66\% false discovery rate corresponds to approximately five to six precautionary alerts per site per year over the independent test period (439 false positives across 10 sites, $\sim$8 years of test data). Since shellfish sampling already occurs on a weekly basis at these sites, a false alarm represents a recommendation to prioritize or expedite sampling---not an automatic harvest closure. This low operational cost of a false alarm, relative to the public health consequence of a missed DA spike, justifies the recall-optimized operating point. The classifier is designed as a decision-support tool that flags weeks warranting heightened attention, not as an autonomous trigger for management action.

\subsubsection{Ensemble Weight Robustness}
\label{sec:weight-robustness}

Since all sites now use single-model configurations (XGBoost-only or RF-only), weight selection reduces to a binary model-selection decision per site. Table~\ref{tab:weight-robustness} (Appendix~\ref{app:weight-robustness}) compares three deterministic strategies on the independent test set: XGBoost at all sites, Random Forest at all sites, and the per-site dev-set selection used in this paper. The per-site strategy ($R^2 = 0.204$) falls between RF-everywhere ($R^2 = 0.194$) and XGB-everywhere ($R^2 = 0.207$), with differences well within bootstrap confidence intervals (all $\Delta R^2 < 0.013$). The oracle upper bound---selecting the better model per site using test-set $R^2$ directly---achieves $R^2 = 0.210$ ($\Delta R^2 = {+}0.006$ over the per-site dev-set selection), confirming that the dev-set model selection loses only negligible performance relative to perfect hindsight. These results confirm that the per-site model selection is competitive with global single-model strategies and that the choice of XGBoost vs.\ Random Forest per site has modest aggregate impact, consistent with the near-identical dev-set performance at the sites where selection was ambiguous.

\subsubsection{Derived Features and Implicit Nonlinearity}

Six derived environmental features (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-engineering}) were designed to encode well-documented ecological relationships \cite{Trainer2020, McKibben2017, Bond2015, McCabe2016}. However, individual feature ablation (Appendix~\ref{app:feature-ablation}) confirmed that all are negligible ($|\Delta R^2| \leq 0.005$), and all have been removed from the final pipeline. This extends beyond derived features to satellite observations: removing chlorophyll-$a$, FLH, SST anomaly, or K490 individually also falls within sampling variability. Notably, removing K490 \textit{improves} $R^2$ by 0.001. This indicates that XGBoost and Random Forest learn these nonlinear relationships implicitly through sequential splits on the raw features, making explicit encoding redundant. The environmental features that remain in the pipeline---SST, BEUTI, PDO, ONI, and Columbia River discharge---contribute through raw input values. The underlying ecological relationships are well-supported; it is their \textit{explicit feature engineering} that is unnecessary given tree-based architectures.

\subsubsection{The Coos Bay Anomaly}

Coos Bay presents the highest MAE (\SI{24.55}{\microgrampeg}) of any site, reflecting extremely high mean DA---more than double any other site---with correspondingly large variance. The model fails to capture the magnitude of DA spikes at this site ($R^2 = -0.039$), a challenge likely requiring dedicated spike-detection approaches. Coos Bay's anomalous DA levels may reflect its unique estuarine setting, where nutrient inputs from the Coos River and tidal dynamics create conditions particularly favorable for sustained \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} blooms.

\subsection{Significance}
\label{sec:discussion-significance}

DATect represents several firsts in DA forecasting:

\textbf{First quantitative DA forecast for the PNW.} Despite decades of monitoring and qualitative forecasting tools \cite{McCabe2023}, no prior system has produced numerical DA concentration predictions for Washington or Oregon. DATect fills this gap with site-specific forecasts at weekly resolution, achieving $R^2 = 0.480$--$0.789$ at all five Washington coast sites on a fully independent test set.

\textbf{ML outperforms baselines where it matters most.} Standard regression models and na\"ive persistence detect fewer than 24\% of the spike transitions that trigger beach closures. The dedicated binary classifier with safe-baseline training detects 65.2\% [95\% CI: 55\%--74\%]---a 2.8$\times$ improvement over na\"ive persistence and 5.3$\times$ over regression thresholding. This is the clearest evidence that ML provides genuine advance warning beyond what persistence or statistical baselines can achieve, specifically on the operationally critical events. No prior HAB forecasting study has specifically targeted below-to-above-threshold transition detection.

\textbf{Data quality as a binding constraint.} The sharp performance split between well-sampled Washington sites and sparsely sampled Oregon sites demonstrates that monitoring data density---not model architecture---is the primary limiting factor for DA forecast skill. This finding has direct implications for monitoring program design: investment in consistent, high-density sampling would likely yield greater forecasting improvements than algorithmic advances alone.

\textbf{Largest geographic and temporal scope.} With 21 years of data across 10 sites spanning $\sim$\SI{600}{\kilo\meter}, DATect uses the longest temporal record and broadest geographic span of any published quantitative DA/\textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} ML study. Prior ML studies typically covered 5--10 years and smaller regions \cite{Alaez2021, Grasso2019, Lane2009}.

\textbf{Leak-free validation as standard.} The nine explicit data-leakage prevention mechanisms, particularly the per-prediction assertion check, establish a rigorous standard for temporal ML validation that addresses the concerns raised by Kapoor and Narayanan \cite{Kapoor2023}.

\subsection{Implications for Coastal Management}
\label{sec:discussion-management}

DATect has direct implications for coastal resource management in the PNW. Currently, razor clam harvest seasons are managed reactively: beaches open when DA levels test below 20~\si{\microgrampeg}, and close when they exceed this threshold. This reactive approach forces managers to make decisions based solely on the most recent test results, without any forward-looking information about whether DA levels are likely to rise or fall in the coming week.

With quantitative one-week-ahead forecasts, managers could: (1) issue precautionary advisories before DA levels exceed regulatory thresholds, providing a safety buffer; (2) plan harvest openings with greater confidence by predicting when DA levels are likely to decline; (3) optimize sampling effort by directing resources to sites where DA is forecast to rise; and (4) communicate risk more effectively to the public using numerical predictions rather than qualitative categories.

The economic value of such forecasts has been estimated for the related PNW HAB Bulletin system \cite{Jin2024}. Quantitative predictions with site-level specificity could provide even greater value by enabling more targeted management decisions, particularly for the \$30+ million annual recreational razor clam fishery in Washington State \cite{Dyson2010, Moore2020}.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion-limitations}

Several limitations should be acknowledged:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Low-skill Oregon sites}: Two of 10 sites (Coos Bay, Newport) have negative $R^2$, and three additional Oregon sites have near-zero skill. This is attributed primarily to sparser, less consistent sampling by the Oregon monitoring program relative to WDFW/Tribal data in Washington, rather than a fundamental oceanographic forecasting barrier. Improved Oregon sampling density would likely yield substantially better model performance.
    \item \textbf{High-risk classification weakness}: The High and Extreme DA categories---the most critical for public safety---achieve F1 scores of only 0.41 and 0.42 respectively, and 13.9\% of Extreme events are misclassified as Low.
    \item \textbf{Per-site tuning generalization}: Per-site configurations were manually tuned on a development set (seed = 42, 20\%). Comparison with seven automated weight selection strategies (Section~\ref{sec:weight-robustness}) shows that no automated method outperforms manual selection on the independent test set, primarily because the $R^2$ surface is flat at high-skill sites and poorly conditioned at low-skill sites. Nevertheless, the per-site feature subsets and hyperparameters were also manually selected and may not generalize optimally to all future data distributions. End-to-end automated tuning with nested cross-validation remains a desirable methodological improvement.
    \item \textbf{Satellite data continuity}: MODIS-Aqua, the primary satellite data source, is approaching end of life. Transition to successor instruments (e.g., PACE) will require recalibration.
    \item \textbf{Forecast horizon}: The 7-day horizon, while aligned with the HAB Bulletin's operational cycle, may be insufficient for some management decisions that require longer lead times for harvest planning and public communication.
    \item \textbf{No real-time ingestion}: The current system relies on pre-computed cache rather than real-time data feeds, introducing potential latency in operational use.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:discussion-future}

Four directions are most promising for extending DATect:

\textbf{Improved Oregon monitoring.} Given that data quality---not model architecture---is the binding constraint, the highest-impact improvement would be denser, more consistent sampling at Oregon sites. Transfer learning from data-rich Washington sites could also improve low-skill site performance without additional monitoring investment.

\textbf{PACE satellite integration.} NASA's PACE mission \cite{Werdell2019} provides hyperspectral ocean color data (350--885~nm at 5~nm intervals) that could enable direct identification of \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} from space. Integrating PACE data could substantially improve DATect's satellite-based features, particularly as MODIS-Aqua approaches end of life.

\textbf{Deep learning and spatial models.} LSTM \cite{Hochreiter1997} or Transformer \cite{Vaswani2017} architectures could capture sequential dependencies more effectively than the current non-sequential ensemble. Graph neural networks that model along-coast DA propagation---where blooms move southward with the California Current---could improve predictions at data-poor sites by transferring information from data-rich neighbors.

\textbf{Molecular forecasting integration.} Brunson et al.\ \cite{Brunson2024} demonstrated that coexpression of DA biosynthesis genes can predict DA one week in advance. Integrating molecular data from Environmental Sample Processors deployed offshore of PNW beaches could complement DATect's satellite and climate-based features.

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

This paper presented DATect, the first quantitative domoic acid concentration forecasting system for the U.S.\ Pacific Northwest coast. Using per-site XGBoost or Random Forest models with interpolated-training data augmentation and nine data-leakage prevention mechanisms, DATect achieves $R^2 = 0.480$--$0.789$ at all five Washington coast sites under strict leak-free validation on a fully independent test set ($n = 2{,}181$). Oregon sites show substantially lower skill, attributed primarily to sparser monitoring data rather than a fundamental forecasting limitation.

The most operationally significant finding is that standard regression models detect fewer than 24\% of the spike transitions that trigger beach closures, while a dedicated binary classifier using safe-baseline training detects 65.2\%---demonstrating that actionable early warning requires a purpose-built transition detector beyond regression. Component ablation confirms that per-site customization is the most impactful design choice ($\Delta R^2 = -0.158$). These methodological contributions---per-site model selection, interpolated training, and leak-free validation---are applicable beyond DA forecasting to any environmental prediction task with sparse, irregular monitoring data. Deployed as a publicly accessible web dashboard, DATect offers coastal managers the first automated, quantitative complement to the qualitative PNW HAB Bulletin.

\section{Features Explored but Removed}
\label{app:dropped-features}
\label{app:derived-features}
\label{app:feature-engineering}

During development, we explored a number of additional features motivated by domain knowledge of harmful algal bloom dynamics. Individual feature ablation (Table~\ref{tab:feature-ablation}) confirmed that none contributed more than $|\Delta R^2| = 0.005$ to ensemble performance, and all have been removed from the final pipeline. We document them here for the benefit of future researchers.

\subsection*{Derived Environmental Features}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Marine heatwave flag}: Binary indicator for SST anomaly $> \SI{1.5}{\degreeCelsius}$, motivated by the 2015 ``Blob'' event \cite{Bond2015, McCabe2016}. $\Delta R^2 = -0.004$.
    \item \textbf{BEUTI nonlinear terms}: $\text{BEUTI}^2$ for the upwelling ``Goldilocks zone'' \cite{Trainer2020} and a relaxation indicator for onshore transport \cite{Trainer2002}. $\Delta R^2 = -0.001$ and $-0.002$.
    \item \textbf{PDO--ONI phase coherence}: Binary flag for co-occurrence of positive PDO and El Ni\~no \cite{McKibben2017}. $\Delta R^2 = -0.002$.
    \item \textbf{\textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} threshold}: Binary indicator for cell counts $> 50{,}000$~cells\,L$^{-1}$. $\Delta R^2 = -0.005$.
    \item \textbf{Fluorescence efficiency}: FLH\,/\,Chl-$a$ ratio as a physiological stress proxy. $\Delta R^2 = -0.001$.
    \item \textbf{K490 quadratic}: $\text{K490}^2$ for turbidity nonlinearity. $\Delta R^2 = +0.001$ (removing \textit{improves} performance).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Satellite Observations}

Several satellite products were downloaded and tested but contributed negligibly:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{MODIS chlorophyll-$a$} and \textbf{chlorophyll-$a$ anomaly}: $\Delta R^2 = -0.004$ and $-0.001$, respectively.
    \item \textbf{MODIS K490} (diffuse attenuation coefficient): $\Delta R^2 = +0.001$ (removing improves performance).
    \item \textbf{MODIS PAR} (photosynthetically active radiation): $< 1\%$ feature importance.
\end{itemize}
Removing all six satellite-derived features simultaneously costs only $\Delta R^2 = -0.003$.

\subsection*{Temporal and Other Features}

Additional temporal encodings (\texttt{quarter}, \texttt{is\_bloom\_season}, sine/cosine of week-of-year, \texttt{days\_since\_start}), a recency feature (\texttt{weeks\_since\_last\_raw}), site coordinates (\texttt{lat}, \texttt{lon}), and rolling-mean features for 8- and 12-week windows all had $< 1\%$ feature importance and were removed.


\section{Web Application Details}
\label{app:webapp}

\subsection*{Architecture and Deployment}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig3_architecture.png}
\caption{DATect system architecture. Data from satellite, climate, hydrological, and biological sources are processed into a unified dataset, fed to the two-model ML ensemble engine, and served through a FastAPI backend to a React dashboard. A pre-computed cache accelerates retrospective evaluations.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure}

The DATect backend serves forecast requests, retrospective evaluation results, and visualization data (correlation heatmaps, sensitivity analyses, spectral analyses, feature importance rankings). A pre-computed cache system stores results from computationally intensive retrospective evaluations as JSON and Parquet files, with an optional Redis backend providing 100$\times$ faster cache reads for production deployments. The retrospective evaluation cache---encompassing all 2,181 validation points across 10 sites and 6 model configurations---was pre-computed on the University of Washington Hyak high-performance computing cluster (\textit{klone} partition, STF allocation). Because each test point requires training a fresh ensemble on its own chronological training subset, the per-anchor computation is embarrassingly parallel and well-suited to HPC execution; running the full retrospective evaluation sequentially on a laptop would require several days, whereas the Hyak cluster completes the computation in approximately 2--3 hours across parallel jobs.

The system is containerized with Docker and deployed on Google Cloud Run, a serverless platform that scales to zero when idle and scales up to three concurrent instances under load. Cold start time is approximately 10--15 seconds. The deployment pipeline uses Google Cloud Build for continuous integration.

\subsection*{Operational Modes}

\textbf{Real-time forecasting mode}: Users select a target date and monitoring site, and DATect trains a fresh model on all available data up to the anchor date (target $-$ 7 days), generating a point prediction, 90\% confidence interval, and risk category classification. An enhanced forecast endpoint simultaneously runs both regression and classification, returning individual ensemble component predictions (XGBoost, Random Forest, and na\"ive separately), a gradient uncertainty plot, and per-category probability distributions.

\textbf{Retrospective analysis mode}: Users select a model type (ensemble, XGBoost, Random Forest, na\"ive, or linear) and task (regression or classification), and the system retrieves pre-computed validation results across all 2,181 test points. Interactive Plotly charts display predicted-vs-actual scatter plots, per-site performance breakdowns, confusion matrices, residual distributions, and time series overlays. Users can filter by site, date range, and DA risk category.

\subsection*{Visualization Types}

Seven interactive visualization types are available through the dashboard:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Correlation heatmaps}: Pearson correlation matrices for all environmental features versus DA at each site
    \item \textbf{Sensitivity analysis}: Three-panel display including absolute Pearson correlations, Sobol first-order indices (when sufficient data are available), and permutation importance via scikit-learn's LinearRegression
    \item \textbf{Time series comparison}: Overlaid DA and \textit{Pseudo-nitzschia} time series showing the temporal relationship between cell abundance and toxin concentration
    \item \textbf{Spectral analysis}: Power spectral density (Welch method) plots revealing dominant periodicities in DA time series
    \item \textbf{Feature contribution waterfall}: SHAP-style breakdown of individual prediction drivers
    \item \textbf{Latitude waterfall plot}: Multi-site DA time series stacked by latitude, enabling visual identification of along-coast DA propagation patterns
    \item \textbf{Interactive map}: Geographic display of current forecast status across all 10 sites
\end{enumerate}

\section{Feature Importance Analysis}
\label{app:feature-importance}

Persistence and observation-order lag features consistently rank among the most important predictors across all sites, confirming the strong autocorrelation in DA time series. At high-skill Washington sites, environmental features (BEUTI, SST, PDO) contribute meaningful additional predictive power, while Oregon sites rely more heavily on persistence features. Note that high feature importance does not imply high marginal contribution: lag features rank highly because the model uses them extensively, but ablation shows they are largely redundant with \texttt{last\_observed\_da\_raw} ($\Delta R^2 = -0.001$; Table~\ref{tab:ablation}).

Key patterns:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Persistence dominance}: \texttt{last\_observed\_da\_raw} and \texttt{da\_raw\_prev\_obs\_1} are the top features at most sites, reflecting slow DA depuration in razor clam tissue.
    \item \textbf{Upwelling signal}: BEUTI ranks highly at coastal upwelling sites, consistent with the upwelling--DA relationship \cite{Trainer2020, McKibben2017}.
    \item \textbf{Climate forcing}: Raw PDO and ONI indices rank in the top 15 features \cite{McKibben2017}. Derived climate features (PDO--ONI phase, MHW flag) were removed after ablation confirmed negligible contribution.
    \item \textbf{Columbia River effect}: \texttt{discharge} ranks highly at northern Washington sites ($> 46.5$\textdegree\,N), consistent with the freshwater plume's influence on coastal stratification.
\end{itemize}
